# Phonetic and Phonemic Analysis of Barranquenho

## 1. Introduction

Barranquenho is a contact language spoken in Barrancos, a small town in the Alentejo region of Portugal on the Spanish border. It arose from prolonged contact between southern Portuguese (Alentejano) and southern Spanish varieties (Extremaduran and Andalusian). Recognized by Portuguese law (Lei nº 97/2021), Barranquenho constitutes a third linguistic system distinct from both Portuguese and Spanish.

Its phonological system is essentially Portuguese-based but displays several highly distinctive features — most notably sibilant aspiration, betacism, loss of final liquids, and diphthong monophthongization — that set it apart from standard Portuguese and make it phonologically unique among Ibero-Romance varieties.

This analysis is based on the *Gramática Básica de Barranquenho* (2025) and the *Convenção Ortográfica do Barranquenho* (2025).

## 2. Phonemic Inventory

### 2.1 Vowel Phonemes

#### 2.1.1 Oral Vowels (Stressed)

| Phoneme | Height | Backness | Rounding | Orthography | Examples |
|---------|--------|----------|----------|-------------|----------|
| /a/ | Open | Central | No | ⟨a, á⟩ | *pássaru* [ˈpasɐɾu] 'pássaro' |
| /ɛ/ | Open-mid | Front | No | ⟨e, é⟩ | *pedra* [ˈpɛdɾɐ] 'pedra' |
| /e/ | Close-mid | Front | No | ⟨e, ê⟩ | *bêju* [ˈbeʒu] 'beijo' |
| /i/ | Close | Front | No | ⟨i, í⟩ | *libru* [ˈlibɾu] 'livro' |
| /ɔ/ | Open-mid | Back | Yes | ⟨o, ó⟩ | *porcu* [ˈpɔɾku] 'porco' |
| /o/ | Close-mid | Back | Yes | ⟨o, ô⟩ | *dotô* [doˈto] 'doutor' |
| /u/ | Close | Back | Yes | ⟨u, ú⟩ | *fumu* [ˈfumu] 'fumo' |

This yields a typical seven-vowel Ibero-Romance system /a ɛ e i ɔ o u/ in stressed position, identical to Portuguese.

#### 2.1.2 Oral Vowels (Unstressed Final)

Barranquenho reduces the seven-vowel system to three vowels in word-final unstressed position, but with a crucial difference from standard Portuguese:

| Phoneme | Realization | Orthography | Examples |
|---------|-------------|-------------|----------|
| /ɐ/ | [ɐ] | ⟨-a⟩ | *mesa* [ˈmezɐ], *terra* [ˈtɛʁɐ] |
| /i/ | [i] | ⟨-i⟩ | *sociedadi* [susiɐˈdadi], *rapazi* [ʁɐˈpazi] |
| /u/ | [u] | ⟨-u⟩ | *altu* [ˈaltu], *porcu* [ˈpɔɾku] |

**Key distinction from standard Portuguese**: Where standard Portuguese reduces final ⟨e⟩ to [ɨ] (a near-close central vowel), Barranquenho raises it fully to [i]. Similarly, final ⟨o⟩ reduces to [u] as in standard Portuguese. There is also a special set of open final vowels [a, ɛ, ɔ] occurring where a final consonant (/-l/ or /-ɾ/) has been deleted: *amabè* [ɐˈmabɛ] 'amável', *açucà* [ɐsuˈka] 'açúcar', *Bitò* [biˈtɔ] 'Vítor'.

#### 2.1.3 Oral Vowels (Pretonic/Post-tonic)

| Phoneme | Orthography | Examples |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| /ɐ/ | ⟨a⟩ | *cadêra* [kɐˈdeɾɐ] |
| /e/ ~ /ɨ/ | ⟨e⟩ | *pequenu* [pɨˈkenu] |
| /i/ | ⟨i⟩ | *pilotu* [piˈlotu] |
| /o/ | ⟨o⟩ | *olibêra* [oliˈbeɾɐ] |

Note: Pretonic ⟨o⟩ maintains the mid [o] quality rather than reducing to [u], unlike much of standard Portuguese. This is a conservative Alentejano trait.

### 2.2 Diphthongs

#### 2.2.1 Oral Diphthongs and Monophthongization

A major feature of Barranquenho is the systematic monophthongization of several Portuguese diphthongs:

| Portuguese diphthong | Barranquenho result | Orthography | Examples |
|---------------------|---------------------|-------------|----------|
| /ej/ | /e/ | ⟨ê⟩ | *bêju* < *beijo*, *primêra* < *primeira*, *casê* < *casei* |
| /ow/ | /o/ | ⟨ô⟩ | *dotô* < *doutor*, *sô* < *sou*, *labô* < *lavou* |
| /ej/ before V | /e/ | ⟨ê⟩ | *cêa* < *ceia*, *idêa* < *ideia*, *rechêu* < *recheio* |
| /aj/ before /ʃ/ | /a/ | ⟨a⟩ | *abaxu* < *abaixo* |

The conjunction *ou* is further reduced to ⟨o⟩: *oitu o nobi* 'oito ou nove'.

#### 2.2.2 Nasal Diphthongs (Stressed Final)

| Diphthong | Orthography | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| /ɐ̃w̃/ | ⟨-ãu⟩ | *fejãu* 'feijão', *comunhãu* 'comunhão', *sãu* 'são' |
| /ɐ̃j/ | ⟨-ãi⟩ | *mãi* 'mãe', *pãi* 'pães' |
| /õj/ | ⟨-õi⟩ | *patrõi* 'patrões', *liçõi* 'lições' |
| /ũj/ → /ũ/ | ⟨-un⟩ | *muntu* 'muito' (reduced) |
| /jɐ̃w/ | ⟨-eãu⟩ | *leãu* 'leão' |

#### 2.2.3 Nasal Diphthongs (Unstressed Final)

The Portuguese unstressed final nasal diphthong [ɐ̃w̃] (as in *cantam*) undergoes a distinctive reduction in Barranquenho to [õ], written ⟨-on⟩:
- *canton* [kɐ̃ˈtõ] 'cantam', *eron* [ˈɛɾõ] 'eram', *ehtabon* [ɨhtɐˈbõ] 'estavam'
- Preterite 3rd pl.: *casaron* 'casaram', *troxeron* 'trouxeram'

The negation adverb *não* oscillates between *nãu* [nɐ̃w] and the reduced form *nan* [nɐ̃].

## 3. Distinctive Phonological Features of Barranquenho

### 3.1 Betacism (/v/ → /b/)

The most striking consonantal feature: Barranquenho does not have the voiceless labiodental fricative /v/. All instances of Portuguese /v/ are realized as [b]:
- *biba* [ˈbibɐ] ← pt. *viva*
- *binhu* [ˈbiɲu] ← pt. *vinho*
- *berdi* [ˈbeɾdi] ← pt. *verde*
- *abó* [ɐˈbɔ] ← pt. *avó*
- *libru* [ˈlibɾu] ← pt. *livro*
- *trabalhaba* [tɾɐbɐˈʎabɐ] ← pt. *trabalhava*

The grapheme ⟨v⟩ is used only in imported words like toponyms: *Valverde*.

### 3.2 Sibilant Aspiration (/s/ → [h] / _C, _#)

The sibilant /s/ in coda position (syllable-final before a consonant or word-finally before a consonant of the following word) is systematically aspirated to [h], parallel to Andalusian Spanish:
- *mehmu* [ˈmɛhmu] ← pt. *mesmo*
- *ehpanhó* [ɨhpɐˈɲɔ] ← pt. *espanhol*
- *Lihboa* [lihˈboɐ] ← pt. *Lisboa*
- *uh meninu* [uh mɨˈninu] ← pt. *os meninos*
- *Deuh* [dewh] ← pt. *Deus*
- *trêh* [tɾeh] ← pt. *três*

In absolute word-final position (pause), the sibilant is deleted entirely:
- *Barrancu* [bɐˈʁɐ̃ku] ← pt. *Barrancos*
- *ehpanhói* [ɨhpɐˈɲɔj] ← pt. *espanhóis*
- *apó* [ɐˈpɔ] ← pt. *após*
- *íamu* [ˈiɐmu] ← pt. *íamos* (1st pl. verbs)

### 3.3 Loss of Final /-l/ in Stressed Syllables

The lateral /l/ in word-final stressed position is regularly deleted:
- *ehpanhó* [ɨhpɐˈɲɔ] ← pt. *espanhol*
- *azú* [ɐˈzu] ← pt. *azul*
- *Brasí* [bɾɐˈzi] ← pt. *Brasil*
- *Isabé* [izɐˈbɛ] ← pt. *Isabel*
- *Natá* [nɐˈta] ← pt. *Natal*
- *numerá* [numɨˈɾa] ← pt. *numeral*

Exception: monosyllables generally preserve /l/: *mal*, *tal*, *cual*.

### 3.4 Loss of Final /-ɾ/ in Stressed Syllables

The tap /ɾ/ in word-final stressed position is regularly deleted (parallel to Andalusian):
- *cantá* [kɐ̃ˈta] ← pt. *cantar*
- *fazê* [fɐˈze] ← pt. *fazer*
- *dotô* [doˈto] ← pt. *doutor*
- *senhô* [sɨˈɲo] ← pt. *senhor*
- *olhá* [oˈʎa] ← pt. *olhar*

Also in unstressed final syllables: *açucà* [ɐsuˈka] ← pt. *açúcar*.
Exception: some monosyllables preserve /ɾ/: *pur* 'por'.

### 3.5 Loss of Etymological h-

The Latin etymological ⟨h⟩ has no phonetic realization:
- *omen* [ˈomɨ̃] ← pt. *homem*
- *aber* [ɐˈbe] ← pt. *haver*

### 3.6 Diphthong Monophthongization

See §2.2.1. The reduction of /ej/ → [e], /ow/ → [o] is systematic and is reflected in the orthography with circumflex accent.

### 3.7 Nasal Diphthong Reduction

The unstressed final [ɐ̃w̃] → [õ] (§2.2.3) is a highly distinctive feature. The nasal diphthong /ũj/ also reduces to [ũ].

## 5. Allophonic Rules and Phonetic Detail

### 5.1 The status of [h]

The glottal fricative [h] in Barranquenho has dual status. As the aspiration product of coda /s/, it is an allophone of /s/. However, in Spanish loanwords (where it represents /x/, e.g., *heringu* < esp. *jeringo*), it functions as a marginal phoneme. The orthography uses ⟨h⟩ for both values.

### 5.2 The /b/ ~ [β] alternation

Like Spanish and unlike standard Portuguese, /b/ may have a fricative/approximant allophone [β] in intervocalic position in rapid speech (*abistá* [ɐβihˈta]). This is particularly evident in words of Spanish origin.

### 5.3 The trill /ʁ/ ~ /r/

The phoneme written ⟨rr⟩ or initial ⟨r⟩ varies between uvular [ʁ] (as in much of Portugal) and alveolar trill [r] (as in rural Alentejano and Spanish). Both are acceptable realizations.
